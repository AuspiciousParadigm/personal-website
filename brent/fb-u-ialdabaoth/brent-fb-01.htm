Claim: whenever someone is "digging in their heels" (refusing to go along with a pressured/incentivized course of action that seems reasonable to onlookers in the moment), it's because they are attending to the long-term or global consequences of extrapolating out the policy question at hand.

The person or persons pressuring them to just go along with whatever may be completely correct that the short-term, local consequence of compliance is net good. But the pressurers are e...ither ignoring or downplaying what the results would be if all similar circumstances were decided according to the same rule.

Example:
"Why don't you just apologize? This whole thing could be over in ten seconds if you just apologized. You don't have to *mean* it, just get the words out."

Thing the person digging in their heels is feeling, but often unable to articulate: Yeah, it would be over, but if we always cave to this kind of coercive pressure—if we always give in and apologize just to smooth things over—then we're incentivizing people to do this more and more and ultimately everything will unravel.

Recommendation: whenever you see someone digging in their heels, imagine that they're being asked to enshrine, in law or custom, the decision at hand. Imagine that their hesitation comes from being asked to commit to following this same decision pattern 1000 more times. I predict this will produce in you an accurate and useful measure of empathy.

Julia Eli John John Brent Divia Rob Raymond Qiaochu Tsvi

EDIT: updating to include a crucial piece from Andrew, slightly paraphrased:

Another piece of the puzzle is that people are usually something like 90% in favor of the action they're being asked to (and refusing to) take, and that there is some component comprising 10% of what's happening that they are refusing to budge on. From the other side, this looks like a baffling "100% against," when actually there's something like a bucket error going on.

When I was 20 or so, I saw a time-lapse video of a barbecue in Texas on YouTube. About halfway through, the Milky Way starts casually making its way across the sky. I was flabbergasted because I had never seen the Milky Way before in my life, and I thought you needed a telescope or something.

And then I realized: if the Greeks were able to name the Milky Way, it must've been visible to the naked eye for most of human history, and it was visible wherever this barbecue was in ...Texas. And the reason I had never seen it was because I had never been somewhere with low enough levels of light pollution.

When I realized this, I felt... well. Imagine being a character in a dystopian YA novel where they all live underground because of some terrible disaster. Somewhere, you've heard some rumors about this thing called "the Sun," but it never seemed very interesting or important. And then one day someone smuggles a video to you. And you hit play. And you see something. And it's bright. And it's beautiful. And this whole time you didn't know.

I felt a little bit like that. It was like part of my birthright as a human being had been taken from me.

(The last time I told this story, Brent Dill immediately responded, "are you free next weekend?" and drove me to Tahoe to see it. Bless his heart.)

---

This is the question that haunts me: what else is missing?

If there was something else beautiful, some other part of my human heritage, that was missing... how would I know? And what would I need to do to get it back?

I think about this question a lot. I have some ideas. Maybe you do too?
