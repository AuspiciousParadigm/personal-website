Kyle Goodman "Likewise, the process of meting out the punishment purges the sense of blame that can keep a Power from directly attending to the underlying mistakes that led to the transgression. Further likewise, the process of witnessing the punishment should purge the sense of blame that might lead the larger social environment to mete out its own punishment, often to the detriment of everyone involved."

I think this is a very essential point to reconsider, and again, it is entirely relative to the entities/powers involved in the crime/sin and how they operate at a cognitive/behavioral/neuropsychological level. However, I am a firm subscriber to the mentality that punishment removes the necessity for someone to correct themselves.

By meting-out punishment to the sin committed, I have found that subordinates tend to re-value the extremity of their crime to meet the extremity of the punishment, and afterwards consider that no further corrective action need be taken by them. Repeated acts of punishment will entrain someone to expect punishment for mis-deeds, even to a fearful level, and albeit that this is a core component to operant conditioning, it is not necessarily the most effective form of conditioning of a human mind in my opinion.

The reason why I say this is that I have experienced a number of experimental consequences, of +/- Reinforcement and +/- Punishment from my mother, who attempted physical punishment once and decided that she was not a fan. Of my entire childhood of "here's a reward for this", or "no you can't go do that" and other variations on those themes, the most memorable and effective experience I had as a child was a lecture given to me by my grandmother: she used logic to rationally explain, over the course of two hours, why I should be selective and careful about the words I choose to use. That memory fondly haunts me to this day as a core learning-experience wherein I was given knowledge about how my actions and my words can affect others. This persists to this day, as a stronger memory than when I was struck on the bottom, AND far more strongly than anything I was ever taught in church. Sure, it may have been a small amount of positive punishment by taking two hours of my childhood time to discuss this, but it was the content that changed me.

To sum all of this into one anecdote of my life, from my experience of my own upbringing, and from my experience in people-management operating a group of friends in event production, the more viable and long-lasting approach to correction comes from rational discussion and education.

TL;DR Punishment removes the feeling of responsibility to self-correct, and making someone feel pain because they committed a sin is far less effective than the results of teaching them how and why their actions(and in some cases non-actions) could be causing pain in most cases.

Marriage, being something considered eternal, was determined based on one's financial wealth, because if you were blessed with money, you would also be going to heaven.

This became a very strict culture of discipline, because a family who wanted their name to be carried-forth, would have to make their children extremely disciplined to carry-out business. Contrary to popular beliefs that the markets were traditionally operated by the Jews, these protestants were some of the original lenders and bankers in American history. We have protestants to thank for the standardized appearance of "business formal", as they shunned luxuries and color during their lives on earth, to demonstrate their humility, and obvious destiny of greater grandeur after-judgement.

I'm not saying it's the only contributing factor, as we come from a multitude of cultures, many of-which were known for strictness and punishment, but again, many of the other strict families came from Catholicism, as-opposed to Protestantism. Read: Irish Catholic, Catholic-Hispanics, and Russian Orthodox. Of course, we wouldn't want to forget Jewish culture--nor should we neglect the contributions made by slavery, but if you had to ask me why we are such big fans of punishment, I would say that the major contributor in America, at-least, has been Protestant Predestiny.

Remember, that all of these cultures came from religions that have been used throughout history to control a population in-place of sufficient military, as kingdoms and empires were grabbing for land. Discipline and punishment, both physical and psychological, were tricks of the trade.
