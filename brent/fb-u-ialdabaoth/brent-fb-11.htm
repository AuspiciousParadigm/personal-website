Damon Max Pourtahmaseb-Sasi In addition to the above answers, bitterness often comes from an intrinsic sense of *unfairness to us in particular.* Recognizing that the world is unfair doesn't make people bitter: we become bitter when we believe that we got dealt the worst hand, repeatedly, while others who appear no better, if not outright worse than us, still win again and again.

It's usually considered a bad trait because in general people have an intuition that on a long enough timeline, unfairness is doled out equally, so anyone who is bitter about the way life has treated them must be focusing too much on the negatives, or just jealous, or whatever. This of course ignores the idea that certain dice rolls, particularly those that effect starting conditions, have long lasting and continuous effects. Anyone who has succeeded despite hardship can easily say "Well, *I* had to overcome a lot to succeed, so I *know* that all it takes is sufficient bootstrapping to overcome the slings and arrows of outrageous fortune," as if "bootstrap pulling" is not itself a trait determined in a number of ways by fortune.

But once in awhile bitterness is validated by people because the bad hand dealt is considered sufficiently atrocious. You often see this with people who lose their family in an accident, or the ability to walk, or get cancer at a young age. Whether bitterness gets validated usually comes down to whether the misfortune is visible enough and large enough to meet the viewer's empathy threshold.

On a purely practical level however, bitterness is like depression in that it's an emotionally draining mood to be around, and so completely apart from whether or not someone thinks the bitterness is justified, they might still not want to be around it.
