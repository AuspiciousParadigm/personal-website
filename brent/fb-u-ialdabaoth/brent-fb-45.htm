Okay, so. Let's construct a new D&D mythology.

You have four 'quadrants' of Celestial Alignment: Lawful Good, Lawful Evil, Chaotic Good, and Chaotic Evil.

On the side of Lawful Good, you have the Devas; while on the side of Lawful Evil, you have the Devils.

...

On the side of Chaotic Evil, you have Demons. And on the side of Chaotic Good, you have... what? Faeries? Totems? Nature-gods? Kami?

Note that Devas and Devils are basically good/evil versions of the same thing, and are very different from Demons or Faeries. If we take that split (Law | Chaos) as the fundamental disjoint, the way original D&D did, then it seems natural to declare Demons and Faeries to be opposing factions of the same thing.

I'm imagining the Devas and Devils having a strongly Hindu aesthetic, with a bit of Chinese and Judeo-Christian symbolism mixed in.

The Faeries and Demons, on the other hand, need to be Norse all the way.

The end result actually looks reasonably similar to 'stock' D&D, from whence came Planescape. Different enough to be its own thing, though, which is cool.

---------------------------------------------
...So, that's one way you can go.
---------------------------------------------

*ANOTHER* interesting way you can go, is to put beings on the ends of the axes, instead of the corners. And then you get a very different flavor.

Putting Gods and their servants on the Good - Evil axis seems straightforward. But then what goes on the Law - Chaos axis?

The last setting idea I tinkered with, which still Has No Name, puts the Undead firmly on the Law side, and very demon-like Djinn on the Chaos side. The Undead don't care for "Good" or "Evil", per se; they just care obsessively about Order and Propriety. And the Djinn don't care for "Good" or "Evil" either; they just care obsessively about Passion and Freedom.

Which means Djinn are basically wish-pushers, trying to hook their marks on fulfilling deeper and deeper passions, always twisting their wishes so that some deeper need will go unfulfilled, so that there'll always be something more for their junkie to chase.

While the Undead maintain massive ancestor-cults, shepherding their still-living descendents along well-cultivated and planned grooves, keeping the wheels of civilization turning.

I'm imagining a Djinn-run civilization to have the aesthetics of mythical Bagdad, full of bazaars and markets and palaces where the most fanciful of things can be purchased for the most dire of costs... where *anything* or *anyone* can be had for the right price. Sort of like Nexus, from Exalted.

Likewise, I'm imagining the Undead-run civilization to have the aesthetics of late-period Egypt, with pyramids and temples and huge public works projects, where the living spend their lives earning their place in the afterlife through hard work, service, and adherence to the bureaucracy.

So, which seems more interesting?

I notice that I tend to be passable at almost anything I try to accomplish. A large part of this is a failure to be shamed by trying things that I've never tried before.

Most people seem more afraid of embarrassing themselves by doing a bad job at something new, than they are curious about how a thing is done. Instead, I notice that most of my focus when trying a thing for the first time is on trying to suss out the 'first principles' behind accomplishing the tasks.

I also n...otice that there's a LOT of social pressure to interpret things as 'badly done', if they were done in an amateur way. This is NOT condusive to levelling people up! People are going to do sloppy, confused work the first time they try things. If you point this out to them, they'll try to cover it up to save face. Minimizing this social pressure, while still giving accurate feedback about where to improve, can give people extra affordance to learn new things.
