Ross Rheingans-Yoo I'm not sure that a ranking of "more powerful" to "least powerful" captures the way that the three interact. If you need metis to use gnosis and you need techne to use metis, then the most-powerful-in-practice may differ from the most-powerful-absent-other-constraints.

A model:
- working on gnosis produces g units of gnosis
- working on metis consumes 1 unit of gnosis and produces m units of metis
- working on techne consumes 1 unit of metis and produces t units of techne
- consumption consumes 1 unit of techne and produces u units of utility.

(Consider extensions: your production ratios may differ from other workers'; units may have "quality" which combines ~multiplicatively across layers; units may have properties that make them easier or harder for certain workers to use; units aren't actually 'consumed'; but if you repeat work done on a unit, you may just get the same successor units.)
