Brent Dill Okay so. There are only four classes: fighter, rogue, wizard, priest.

Each class has four different roles: striker, defender, controller, leader.

Fighter: warrior, knight, ranger, marshall.

Rogue: assassin, acrobat, thief, bard.

Wizard: evoker, abjurer, conjurer, artificer.

Priest: zealot, paladin, theurge, cleric.

Then each class gets a customization choice: fighters pick a fighting style; rogues pick an expertise; wizards pick a school of magic; priests pick a faith.

Some of these classes can fork in interesting directions - for example, a priest of a Primal faith is going to be different enough from a 'normal' priest that you could reasonably rename the roles (zealot = barbarian, paladin = warden, theurge = druid, cleric = shaman). But the underlying mechanics will still be there.

Jake Coble As a person, the thing you want is to win games. You need some things to win games. First of all, you have to not be dead. Second of all, you need resources. You need at least *some* resources, but the more resources the better. Not only does it increase your odds of winning games that other people have set up, but it lets you set up games that you can win.

There's a whole metagame here: managing your resources and managing the games. It's the root game. There really isn't a win condition (untrue in the context of certain religions that posit a cosmic game). There's just a lose condition: you lose when you can no longer win any more games.

The easiest way to lose the metagame is to be dead. You've unambiguously lost at that point. So one of the most popular methods of making someone lose the metagame (and therefore any games nested within it) is to make them dead. Fortunately, societies now tend to put people into cages so they can't win anymore games for a while, but it's still really popular to just make them dead.

So say you're playing a poker game. You want to win this game because games are fun to win – that's what you're here for after all. But sometimes you're trying to win a game because you want to win *another* game. Maybe you need the money from the poker game to buy fancy clothes because fancy clothes are key pieces in your sex-game strategy. Maybe you need the money from the poker game to win the politics game. Maybe you need the money to buy food or pay off a creditor so you don't lose the metagame. In any case, the poker game isn't the only game you've got on your mind.

Oh, and you have a pistol with you.

Now, it's often a bad idea to abuse the metagame. Iterated prisoners dilemmas, TDT, and related handwaving. You're not going to get invited to a lot more poker games if too many of your hosts end up dead. But let's say in this case that you're not a professional poker player and you're never going to sit at another poker table for the rest of your life. Let's also assume that you own the metagame in this context: you've got the only gun and your getaway horse is right outside. Law-enforcement is pretty bad in your time period and you know you can get away with it.

A lot of the stuff we do is automatic. You might end up sitting down at the poker table and losing the poker game because it didn't even occur to you that you could win the metagame. Because you're so accustomed to playing games and you're so unaccustomed to winning the metagame, pulling out your pistol and just taking the money isn't even on the list of options your brain presents to you.

I believe that's what Brent is talking about here. I just made it more long winded because it seems there was confusion.
