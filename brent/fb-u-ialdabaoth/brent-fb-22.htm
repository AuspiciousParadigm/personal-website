Hey guys. Maybe this is too meta, but I just want to point out something about this whole rationalist custom of making bets:

You aren't betting that event N will occur. You're betting that *your opponent will agree that event N will occur*.

I always, always reserve the right to refuse bets, or adjust the odds of bets, based on who's taking the other side. It is COMPLETELY plausible that I could bet with one of you that the sun will rise tomorrow, bright golden light can pour... through my window at 7:15 AM, I can grab you by the shoulders and POINT AT THE SUN, and you can shrug and say "optical illusion. Pay me."

And that NEEDS to be factored into the betting odds.

Look at your social capital and raw power, compared to theirs. Use that to determine what they lose if they weasel out of the bet. Look at the likelihood that they can convince others - who you might expect to shame them for reneging - to take their side and shame YOU instead, even though all evidence points to your side of the bet occurring.

Bets do not occur in a political vacuum. Ultimately, you are owed, and ONLY owed, what you can force someone to pay.

Which means that if you notice that you have a lot of social capital, you should be making a lot of bets and then enforcing payment regardless of what the "evidence" shows. Take advantage of it. Demonstrate your raw power. Bleed those under you dry.

To hell with the facts.

This is a frustration I have with science journalism:

"In quantum mechanics, however, a particle can exist not just in distinct states, like the heads and tails of a coin, but in a so-called superposition — roughly speaking, a combination of those states. In other words, a quantum bit, or qubit, can be not just in the binary state of 0 or 1, but in a superposition of the two."

That. Isn't. Other. Words. You just said "superposition" twice, and claimed that one of those times ...was defining or explaining it. You then proceed to talk for 15 more paragraphs about quantum physics as if I understand it, when I'm pretty sure *you* don't. How about:

"In quantum mechanics, however, a particle can exist not just in distinct states, like the heads and tails of a coin, but in a so-called superposition — roughly speaking, a combination of those states. In other words, a quantum bit, or qubit, can be not just in the binary state of 0 or 1, but in a 'superposition' that describes how likely it is to read as 0 and how likely it is to read as 1. This superposition can be 'entangled' with other qubits, so that the likelihood of 0 or 1 is related in complicated ways to the likelihood that other qubits read as particular values - with the understanding that if one particular qubit is definitively read as 0 or 1, all the bits entangled with it necessarily 'collapse' to better-defined values."
