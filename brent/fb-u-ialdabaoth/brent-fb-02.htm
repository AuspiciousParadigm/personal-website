Ben Hoffman I *think* I strongly disagree with a key part of what you're saying, but I'm not sure because I'm not sure what the core of what you're saying is, and that's part of the problem here. But this seems pretty whiggish to me in a way that isn't actually operationalizable, where attempts to operationalize it will end up allowing some parties (by means not fully specified) to demand infinite amounts of interpretive labor from others, asymmetrically.

The workable version of this treats interpretive labor as a cost. Yes, you *are* losing something when you take the time to understand the position of someone who seems to you to be arguing in bad faith. You're spending down some of the one irreplaceable resource you have: time. This is why "don't feed the trolls" is a thing - albeit a completely inadequate slogan, since there's not a clear adequate standard. Sometimes we need to take on that risk, but it *is* a risk.

I agree that "they're evil" is not an explanation. It's just a bid to scapegoat people, and it's really annoying that this is so prevalent. But I don't agree that people are always or usually doing things for reasons, or that their stated reasons are always or usually a good account of their real reasons if they have them. (As Zack keeps pointing out, self-reports are important evidence but shouldn't be the same thing as canonical reality). Sometimes behaviors are better explained as strategies selected for in a particular ecology, than as microeconomic responses to incentives; evaluating incentives is not a free action, it tags you as a particular sort of agent, which often commits you to a particular coalition, not necessarily an appealing one.

Duncan Sabien In any mismatch of models (that wants to be prevented in the future) there's always the question: where should the intervention lie?

For instance, if you take an action and I become upset, it's possible that the future fix is you not taking those actions, or me not becoming upset, or something in between.

If an author writes a scene, and that scene is misinterpreted, it's possible that the fix is for the author to improve their writing, or for the readers to improve their reading (such as by delivering the story more narrowly to an intended audience that will not make the mistake), or something in between.

I think it would be highly useful to, as you say, find better ways to think about and communicate the memeplex that Ben is working with. It's produced novel insight that's been useful for a lot of people, and it definitely has significant non-overlap with my own ideas.

But there's this thing where, as Ben attempts to work with and communicate that memeplex, things have gone wrong in the same way more than once. In my own particular case, there've been a lot of statements which Ben later clarified and explained but which, on the surface, had as their most likely interpretation:

- Ben thinks Duncan is physically dangerous to other members of the Berkeley rationalist community
- Ben thinks that there is literally no other explanation for United Airlines' choking of a passenger than the United Airlines entity wanting to flex its muscles and show how strong it is
- Ben thinks that Duncan wants to round people up and put them in ghettos

Ben did not believe any of those things. I'll repeat, for emphasis, so it doesn't sink into people's heads: Ben did *not* believe any of those things, at least not in so raw and straw a form. But those are only three of the (perhaps five or six?) times that things have gone awry with *just me.* I'd be genuinely surprised to find that those five or six are the only instances of Ben being misread in a similar (and, I claim, foreseeable and predictable) fashion.

1) I don't actually know if that's a good thing to hope for in the first place; if that world is actually better. It seems to provide a *lot* more cover and plausible deniability for bad actors, because it makes motte-and-bailey an *extremely* available move.

2) In practice, in my case in particular, it feels like Ben at least is asking for that world asymmetrically, i.e. often *explicitly* taking the least charitable interpretation or most threatening extrapolation of the things that I say and do, and then being frustrated or dismayed when I react as if the normal face value interpretation of what he says, is what he meant. For example, I'll advocate for the children's game punch bug, Ben will make a comment about me being willing to ghettoize people, and then when I go on the defensive, Ben will express frustration at how hard it is to communicate and how the cost of interpretive labor is making him want to withdraw entirely. This seems unfair to me. It seems like the problem is not on my end.

(As another example, Ben noted his strong personal reaction to the word "Volkswagen" in one comment, and then later expressed open surprise that people would be triggered by, and take very seriously, the word "ghetto.")

So when I hear that you interpreted what Ben had to say just fine, I don't know what to do with that. Perhaps it's a sign that you're Doing It Right, just like Ben, and that someday the rest of us will catch up, or perhaps it's a sign that you just happen to be on the rare right wavelength to catch what he's trying to get across, or perhaps in this case that *was* the dominant interpretation, but I was (hopefully understandably) betting on a different prior because of our previous interactions.

I don't know. But personally, I suspect that there's something *about the memeplex itself* that is driving these persistent model mismatches. I think that something about the kinds of frames that Ben is using, and the kinds of conclusions he's drawing from those frames, is causal in these miscommunications.
