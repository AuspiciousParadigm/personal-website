Mackenzie Lee Not speaking with a lot of confidence here, just exploring a possible explanation for part of the problem:

Food is acknowledged as a need, because you will die without it. People have about, what, a month? of trying to pretend that they don't need it to survive. Sex, affection, and touch...not so much. There's a socially acceptable way for a scrupulous or compassionate person to provide food to starving people, and even though some people have contempt for those who take such charity, there's very little contempt for those who offer it. This isn't true for affection and sex.

If I openly said "I'm going to have sex with Joe because he's lonely and I want to help him feel less lonely," *I'd* be the target of contempt for doing that openly, *Joe* would be even more the target of contempt for needing "charity," and *I would be making it worse for Joe* by explicitly saying that I was providing affection out of altruism because I would be making it even more obvious that he needed the charity. So: being open about what I'm doing for Joe is uncomfortable because I incur social costs and make his costs worse. *Not* being open about what I'm doing for Joe is bad for both of us, because I have to invent a plausible excuse for giving him affection and sex, AND I can't be open with him about the limits of my "charity," which leads to horrible, painful misunderstandings.

If a well-meaning, compassionate, or scrupulous person faces a problem where all of the solutions suck (either take the social hit and maybe hurt Joe more, or don't help Joe, or try to help Joe without communicating what's going on), they often get *mad* rather than taking the least-terrible option. People get mad at the trolley problem for the same reason! I don't think this is what's going on in every case, but for some people, in some situations like what you describe, they're getting mad in the same way. Which can turn into "Well, fuck you for asking." And we have an easy narrative now for guys who are saying that they need sex to survive/thrive: entitled. "Fuck you for asking, entitled pig, no one owes you sex."

If it's any reassurance, I think sex/affection is a very reasonable thing to say that you need, and it is not the same as genuinely acting entitled. I don't even think "acting entitled" is as bad as it's made out to sound. There's definitely some sort of terrible behavior where people act as if their needs should be prioritized without any investment on their part, where they're trivializing the costs that other people incur in giving it to them...but shit, they still need it.

On the difference between "deserving" and "being owed," yeah, that's important, you can say things like "I deserve to eat every day" and people don't *necessarily* interpret that as "I'm going to take your food by force and give it to other people." But when sex comes up, they may hear "I deserve sex and affection" and think "They're going to take sex and affection by force." I don't know how to tell people in a productive way that they're wrong to do that.

EDIT: I didn't finish this properly. To actually directly address your status, I think that a lot of people are *flinching* and lashing out. I question the reasonableness of telling someone who says "I need sex" that they should "stop acting like they're owed sex." I believe it's an emotional response, not a good argument or an honest observation. There is a difference between needing sex, attention, and affection, and acting "entitled" to it, but I also think that a lot of the "entitlement" framing is people trying to avoid discomfort. Getting mad at trolleys.

A formal model of power exchange and consent:

0. Power exchange is not exclusive to kink, and is not fundamentally a sexual activity. It was first reified in a kink context, but ultimately most primates negotiate power exchanges as part of their lives on a day-to-day basis. Choosing to work for someone in exchange for pay is a power exchange. Choosing to obey the law is a power exchange. Choosing to interact with a company's customer service is a power exchange.

1. Power exc...hange, formally, is any situation where one person gives another the right to impose constraints on their future decisions, in exchange for other considerations. The one who receives the right to impose constraints is the "Dominant" partner, while the one who receives the other considerations is the "submissive" partner, for the duration of the constraints. (One notes the difference between 'submissive' and 'submitting'; one is a state, while the other is an action.)

2. Consent is not about agreement. One can say "yes", even enthusiastically, to a power exchange, and still be violating their sovereignty. Consent is about:
- A) Being consciously aware of the BATNA
- B) Feeling unconstrained to choose the BATNA or the power exchange
- C) Legitimately believing that the power exchange is the better deal.

If one feels like either side "has" to be taken, for reasons of morality or fear, then what is happening is not a power *exchange*; what is happening is a coercive transfer of power.

3. Whether consent can be revoked midstream is an ongoing ethical concern. Without the ability to revoke consent midstream, abuse is statistically inevitable. (Not in any single case, but over the course of all power exchanges without midstream revokation, a certain percentage WILL inevitably be abusive.) On the other hand, if consent can be revoked midstream, then trust is impossible. (See libertarians' musings on 'contract law' for why this is true.)

4. When the situation is emotionally charged and follows well-worn instinctual paths - such as negotiating for sex - stopping to hammer all this out can "totally kill the mood". So culture provides a set of mores and expectations, so that people can follow ready-made scripts rather than sign formal contracts just to grind pelvises together.

However, this makes these ready-made scripts into Chesterton's Fences, where attacking them in the interest of improving women's access to the BATNA actually destroys the entire utility of the social script. And the alternative scripts proposed have not yet been "charged up" with mana by our current cultural narrative disseminators, so they aren't "hot" and "romantic" in the way that the old, patriarchally-fraught patterns are.

I'm not sure what to do about this.
