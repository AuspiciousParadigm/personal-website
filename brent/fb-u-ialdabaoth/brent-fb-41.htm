Perry Metzger
October 11, 2017

On open secrets:

Apparently Harvey Weinstein's behavior was well known for years without anyone really doing anything about it.

Jokes about Catholic Priests mol...esting altar boys were prevalent for years and years before anyone really investigated if anything was behind them, so much so that I, a non-Catholic, was aware of them growing up.

Stories about the NYC private trash hauling business being controlled by the Mafia were common among ordinary New Yorkers for decades before anyone in law enforcement decided to bother investigating. They of course were entirely true.

I will perpetuate a bit of impunity by noting that a couple of decades ago, I dated a woman who worked in a particular non-profit organization and who told me that a famous important person attached to that organization was well known within the organization to be a pedophile and that there had been repeated coverups and payouts to prevent this from leaking. Her co-workers seemed to joke about it too. No one to my knowledge has ever done anything about this.

I can mention many, many more such cases. Several of the above ones were sexual in nature, but many or even most of the ones I can name (like certain corrupt practices well known to happen in certain law enforcement agencies, or certain sorts of routine lying in certain industries, and yes, I'm being vague here) aren't.

I can name instances that range from things that are fairly confined (particular individuals in certain companies being known for corruption) to very general (instances where it seems that millions of people were aware of some very bad thing that none the less goes for decades before anyone gets arrested.)

It appears that it is often the case that open secrets circulate in communities or even in the world at large, where lots and lots of people are aware of something bad happening and yet almost no one does anything about it.

I find this fascinating.

Some of the explanation for what is going on is surely fear — after all, there is reason, if you're in a small organization, not to be identified as the person who turned a prominent member of the organization in. But surely in many other cases, it can't possibly be fear as such, because the malfeasance is so far detached from the lives of of the literally hundreds of thousands or millions of people who have heard of it, many of whom must surely be in law enforcement.

One wonders what this says about human psychology, and what knock-on effects it has on politics, organizational behavior, and even daily interactions.

Cycle I'm really damn tired of:

I do a thing, for free, for the joy of it.

It's a thing other people find extremely useful. They seek me out to do the thing.

...

I *love* doing it.

But I'm starting to run low on money.

I notice that there are other people who do the thing I do for money. I tell myself, "I know! I'll set myself up to get PAID to do the thing!"

Immediately, I start worrying. "Am I really good at the thing? And am I actually doing it because it's my calling, or was this whole thing just a cynical bid for money and attention?"

I imagine all my friends telling me that I'm not good enough at the thing, and that I'm just doing it because I want money, attention, and status - and that I don't *DESERVE* them, because I'm just doing the thing for money, and always was.

Immediately, I start worrying. "Am I really good at the thing? And am I actually doing it because it's my calling, or was this whole thing just a cynical bid for money and attention?"

Soon, I stop enjoying doing the thing, even for free. Whenever I do the thing, I worry that my friends KNOW that I never really did the thing for noble reasons; that I'm actually just a scammer at heart, and anyways, that I was never good at doing the thing.

So I fall into a depression, until I find a NEW thing to do, for free, for the joy of it. And then other people find the thing I do useful...
