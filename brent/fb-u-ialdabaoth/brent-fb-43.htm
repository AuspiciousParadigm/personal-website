Brent Dill Incentive Slope:

Imagine you have a bunch of agents playing a multiplayer iterated game, who may or may not be in a game-theoretic equilibrium. The "incentive slope" is the direction that short-term incentives pull each agent towards. For example, in an IPD, the one-step "incentive slope" is towards Defection.

In more complex games, the Incentive Slope might be more complex than this, with multiple actions feeling more or less aligned with or against the incentive gradient.

Different actions by different agents can change each others' incentive slopes, creating a shifting landscape.

Most humans, in social interaction, naively follow their local incentive slope with very little look-ahead. Many environments actually create, as part of the incentive slope, a discouragement against deep look-ahead - calling it "manipulative" or "machiavellian". That said, following the local incentive slope as a hill-climbing optimizer will NOT tend to reach Pareto-optimal solutions; you need agents capable of deep look-ahead for that.

When you attend to the local incentive slopes, and attend to the influence you have on them, you can set up 'breadcrumb trails' of local incentive for the less aware agents to follow - this can be to their benefit or their detriment.

Coordination failures often happen because people designed systems assuming that everyone would see and jump to the correct Pareto-optimal spaces, rather than attending to the fact that people start in different positions in the state-space, and those positions can have incentive slopes that lead into arbitrary attractors.

Brent Dill Sacred Value:
An actual intrinsic (as opposed to instrumental) value that a human-like agent holds, that their behavior *actually* seeks to protect. (As opposed to a claimed or signaled intrinsic value, or even an actual intrinsic value that they do not have any effective ways to maximize/satisfice). A bona-fide Sacred Value is something that a human-like agent WILL react to threats against, and their behavior tracks it as a bona-fide incentive.

Put in more CFAR terms: A Sacred Value is an intrinsic value that automatically goal-propagates.

Brent Dill SNAFU telephone:

The "SNAFU Principle" is a concept created or popularized by Robert Anton Wilson. It reads:

"True communication is possible only between equals, because inferiors are more consistently rewarded for telling their superiors pleasant lies than for telling the truth."

The SNAFU principle is the idea that there is an Incentive Gradient against epistemic clarity, wherever there is a Power Differential. (And that Status Differentials inherently contain a kind of Power Differential).

"SNAFU telephone" is what you get when you are two or more steps away from a source of information, and each step contains a Power Differential:

---

Brent Dill Hot-Swappable Ontology:

Hooo boy. This one is complex and fiddly, and I can only really *gesture* at it.

A hot-swappable ontology is an ontology that is designed to be adopted or abandoned quickly, and that has translation layers into other hot-swappable ontologies.

One might use a hot-swappable version of Newtonian mechanics to work out an orbital solution to exit the solar system, and then "swap out" with GR to work out an intercept with a neighboring star. In this case, there is explicit math which does the job of translating facts within one ontology into the other ontology.

Hot-swappable ontologies are *extremely* useful for sociology. Robert Sapolsky's "Behave" is an excellent example of someone using hot-swappable ontologies - he'll go through a behavioral scenario, examining all the game-theoretic incentives behind it (ontology: "humans are rational agents") - and then dive into the neurochemistry motivating an individual human (ontology: "humans are organic machines"), then dive into the evolutionary neurobiology (ontology: "humans are adaptation-executors in an evolutionary fitness environment"). Each of these ontologies need to be hot-swappable, in the sense that behavior at the social level needs to *mean something*, gears-wise, at the neurochemical level, or you're talking about correlated things instead of talking about different frames of the same thing.

Hot-swappable ontologies are also useful for investigation and conflict resolution. If two people have very different reports of how a conflict happened, accepting each of their narrative ontologies as internally consistent can lead to taking one side or the other. If instead, you can find all the ways that a fact can be valid within both ontologies, and use that to work out a general translation algorithm between facts in one ontology and facts in the other, you can facilitate communication between them.

Brent Dill Ur-Ontology:

The thing that System 1 does implicitly, that draws our attention to certain clusters of inputs and calls them a single "perception". The place where priors come from. The thing that generates the fundamental attribution error, as a consequence of having a Theory of Mind at all. The base-level sensory organizational schema that our organic hardware/firmware either comes installed with, or develops naturally in response to its environment without any conscious thought involved.

Many people have a concept that Michael Arc calls "Ontologically Basic Evil". The idea that a thing just "is" Good or Bad, and you *know* it in your gut, is coming from your Ur-Ontology. Often, because these Ur-Ontological feelings are so strong, we'll come up with lots of ontological justifications for *why* they are true, and believe that the underlying opinion came from our ontology.

(Example: Listen to certain people talk about how 'inherently disgusting' homosexuality is, and then listen to all the very rigorous-sounding examples they come up with. Then notice that the disgust arises *independent* of the reasons given for the disgust.)

Brent Dill NOTE: I most often use the term 'Mana' to refer to a depletable resource, NOT a static trait. That said, people have very different capacities in that trait. Think of "Mana" as being very like "Willpower" (and in fact, "Willpower" is one of the more recognizable kinds of Mana.)

Kinds of Mana:
- Money
- Willpower
- Social Capital
- Secret Knowledge
- Physical Stamina
- Confidence
- Attention

Basically, "Mana" is any kind of resource that you *ACTUALLY CONTROL* and can devote to solving a particular problem. Mana is VERY context-dependent. It's the fully general 'resource' trait, contextualized towards the goal you are actually trying to accomplish.

Note: In most human endeavors, the biggest sources of Mana are confidence and attention. This is because your actual control over your other resources (money, willpower, social capital, etc.) depend on your confidence and the quality and quantity of attention pointed at you. Most occult "magick" is about controlling confidence and attention, so as to make all your other resource expenditures more effective.

ALRIGHT LISTEN THE FUCK UP:

Let's say someone asks people for advice - say, publicly. You are one of the people who offer advice.

They then reject your advice - maybe they even give reasons why!

...

Don't get all offended. NEVER say "well if you aren't going to follow my advice, why did you ask?" This pretty much disqualifies you from ever giving any kind of advice to anyone, ever.

When someone asks for advice with a problem, they have some prior probabilities assigned to what the solution to the problem looks like. They're actually asking, "what are other people's hypotheses and probability distributions about the solution-space of this problem?"

Provide solution-candidates. Then the querant (querant? is that a term?) will update their probability that your solution-candidate is the correct one, based on the strength of their prior about that solution-candidate, AND the strength of their trust in you as a Bayesian reasoner in this domain, AND the strength of their trust in your evidence.

If they then reject your solution-candidate, it's because they either have a hypothesis that your evidence is flawed, OR they have a hypothesis that your reasoning is flawed.

"Well, you're a doodoohead for not believing me, and you must not REALLY want to solve your problem!" is NOT a valid move in the Bayesian cooperation game.

(That said, sometimes it IS the correct hypothesis; but not NEARLY at the rates that people invoke it.)
