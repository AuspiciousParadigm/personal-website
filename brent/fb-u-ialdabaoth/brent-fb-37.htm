Daniel Powell The error is that people double-count evidence. That an argument seems reasonable at first is evidence in favor of it, because arguments that seem unreasonable at first are more likely to be unsound than ones that don't, and that an argument is being made by somebody with a history of making sound arguments is also evidence that it is sound,but those two pieces of evidence are not independent of each other. And the intuitive human interaction with two pieces of evidence is faulty even when dealing with unrelated evidence.

Plus the actual human credence function isn't based on the soundness of argument historically made by the source, it's closer to the level of agreement with the conclusions of arguments made by that person.
